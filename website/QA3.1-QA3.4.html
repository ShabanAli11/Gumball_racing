3.4 Describe how you would go about testing digital solutions.

After using the handiest tool at my disposal (Google) I was able to find a website that broke down testing digital solutions to thirteen points which are: - Target market / audience which is when looking at which devices to test against, one must understand what devices the target market is expected to use. Different countries, types of people, including demographics (age brackets) etc will use different devices to get to your site. You must understand where the majority of risk is and look to reduce that risk by testing on the most popular platforms. Existing breakdown of data / device usage which is to check If your site is already in existence, analytics software, such as Google Analytics, IBM Digital Analytics or Adobe Analytics can be utilised to understand what devices and software are used to access your site. Check your website to see compatibility on different browsers across different devices, although it is standard behaviour for the user to utilise the browser which is installed with the device, sometimes users will install different software to browse with. Let the previous two points drive your testing on this, but it may also be prudent to install different browsers on your devices and test using those. The type of journey a customer takes through your site, when testing a customer journey, it can be useful to break up the flow and try to complete it using different devices. For example, for a reset password journey, you could trigger the password reset from a desktop device, but complete the password reset from a mobile phone. Test against a ‘base browser’ as different browsers can produce different results when testing. This is then used as the source of truth when it comes to comparing behaviours of the system across different devices. Are your browsers under Warranty (browsers, versions, operating systems)? Consider what devices, operating systems and browsers are actually under warranty. Often digital companies will state which will be covered under a contract warranty, which makes sense when there are so many browser types, devices and versions out in the marketplace. By only covering what is under warranty, you will not waste time and resources on testing combinations which are not going to be covered. Often, during the development of a website, error messages, pop ups and pop outs (where a new tab is created within the browser) are used. Development of these for different browsers and screen sizes can sometimes be missed and often bears fruit when testing. Execute Bug Hunt & Regression Test cycles This is where, as a test team, we run through a number of user journeys across our available devices. We do this methodically, at the same time, to ensure that we can identify issues as you go. Normally, the team would agree on an amount of time to spend on this. Execute tests on Hardware (where possible) and or software emulators. Test when device is on different networks (3G, 4G, Wi-Fi). Stay in touch with market trends (new browsers, new versions, usability stats) And finally have a final beta test copy you can hand out to a few people to test and get feedback on from an outside source.
